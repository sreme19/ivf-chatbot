## IVF Process and Timeline

### What is IVF?
IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation) is a fertility treatment where eggs are collected from the ovaries and fertilised with sperm in a laboratory. The resulting embryo is then transferred to the uterus. It is one of the most common and well-established assisted reproductive technologies available today.

The process typically takes 2–6 weeks per cycle and involves several carefully coordinated stages. Many people feel overwhelmed at first, but your care team will guide you through every step.

### What are the stages of an IVF cycle?
A typical IVF cycle involves these main stages:
1. **Ovarian stimulation** — Hormone injections stimulate the ovaries to produce multiple eggs over 8–14 days
2. **Monitoring** — Regular ultrasound scans and blood tests track follicle development
3. **Egg retrieval (egg pickup)** — A minor procedure to collect mature eggs from the ovaries
4. **Fertilisation** — Eggs are combined with sperm in the laboratory
5. **Embryo development** — Fertilised eggs are monitored for 3–5 days as they develop
6. **Embryo transfer** — One or more embryos are placed into the uterus
7. **Two-week wait** — Waiting period before a pregnancy test

Each stage is important, and your doctor will personalise the approach based on your specific situation.

### How long does IVF take?
A single IVF cycle typically takes 4–6 weeks from the start of medication to the pregnancy test. However, the overall journey — including consultations, tests, and potentially multiple cycles — can take several months. It is completely normal to feel impatient or anxious during this time. Many people find it helpful to take things one step at a time.

## Injections and Stimulation

### What injections are used in IVF?
IVF typically involves hormone injections to stimulate the ovaries. These are usually self-administered using a small pen-like device and are given under the skin (subcutaneously). Most people find them manageable after the first few days.

The specific medications, doses, and schedules are determined entirely by your doctor based on your individual response and medical history. Please do not adjust your medication without consulting your doctor.

### Will the injections hurt?
Most people describe the injections as a mild sting or pinch. The needles are very fine. Some people experience bruising or tenderness at the injection site, which is normal. If you experience significant pain, swelling, or redness, contact your clinic.

### What is ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS)?
OHSS is a condition where the ovaries respond too strongly to stimulation medications. Mild OHSS is relatively common and causes bloating and discomfort. Severe OHSS is rare but requires medical attention.

Symptoms to watch for include severe abdominal pain, significant bloating, nausea, vomiting, or difficulty breathing. If you experience these, contact your clinic immediately.

## Egg Pickup Procedure

### What happens during egg retrieval?
Egg retrieval (also called egg pickup or OPU — ovum pick-up) is a minor surgical procedure performed under sedation or light anaesthesia. A thin needle is guided through the vaginal wall using ultrasound to collect eggs from the follicles in the ovaries.

The procedure usually takes 20–30 minutes. Most people feel some cramping or discomfort afterwards, which typically resolves within a day or two. You will need someone to accompany you home.

### How many eggs will be collected?
The number of eggs collected varies greatly between individuals and depends on factors like age, ovarian reserve, and response to stimulation. Not all eggs collected will be mature, and not all mature eggs will fertilise successfully. Your doctor will discuss realistic expectations with you based on your specific situation.

### What happens after egg retrieval?
After egg retrieval, you may experience mild cramping, bloating, or spotting for a few days. Rest is recommended on the day of the procedure. Your clinic will contact you with updates on fertilisation and embryo development over the following days.

## Embryo Transfer

### What is an embryo transfer?
Embryo transfer is the final step of the IVF process where one or more embryos are placed into the uterus using a thin, flexible catheter. It is usually a simple, painless procedure that does not require anaesthesia — similar to a cervical smear.

You may be asked to have a full bladder for the procedure to help with ultrasound guidance. Most people can return to normal activities the same day.

### How many embryos will be transferred?
The number of embryos transferred depends on your age, embryo quality, and medical history. In many cases, a single embryo transfer (SET) is recommended to reduce the risk of multiple pregnancies. Your doctor will discuss the best approach for your situation.

### What should I do after embryo transfer?
After embryo transfer, you can resume most normal activities. There is no evidence that bed rest improves outcomes. Light activity is generally fine. Avoid strenuous exercise, alcohol, and smoking. Take any medications prescribed by your doctor as directed.

## Two-Week Wait

### What is the two-week wait?
The two-week wait (2WW) is the period between embryo transfer and the pregnancy test. It is often described as one of the most emotionally challenging parts of IVF. Feelings of anxiety, hope, and uncertainty are completely normal.

### How can I cope during the two-week wait?
There is no single right way to cope. Some people find it helpful to stay gently busy, lean on supportive friends or family, or connect with others going through IVF. Others prefer quiet and rest. Be kind to yourself — whatever you feel is valid.

Avoid taking home pregnancy tests too early, as they can give misleading results. Wait for the blood test your clinic has scheduled.

### What symptoms might I feel during the two-week wait?
Some people experience symptoms like light spotting, cramping, breast tenderness, or fatigue — these can be caused by the progesterone medication and do not reliably indicate whether the cycle has succeeded or not. Try not to over-interpret physical sensations during this time.

## Failed IVF Cycles

### What happens if IVF doesn't work?
A failed IVF cycle is a deeply painful experience, and it is completely understandable to feel grief, anger, sadness, or confusion. These feelings are valid and deserve space.

A failed cycle does not mean IVF will never work for you. Many people go on to have successful pregnancies after one or more unsuccessful cycles. Your doctor will review what happened and discuss next steps, which may include adjustments to the protocol, further investigations, or other options.

### Why do IVF cycles fail?
IVF cycles can fail for many reasons, including embryo quality, implantation issues, or factors that are not yet fully understood by science. In many cases, there is no clear explanation, which can be frustrating. It is important to know that a failed cycle is not your fault.

### How soon can I try again after a failed cycle?
The timing of a subsequent cycle depends on your physical and emotional recovery, as well as your doctor's recommendations. Most clinics suggest waiting at least one full menstrual cycle before trying again. Take the time you need — there is no rush.

## Miscarriage

### What is a miscarriage in the context of IVF?
A miscarriage after IVF — sometimes called a biochemical pregnancy or early pregnancy loss — is the loss of a pregnancy before it is viable. It is a heartbreaking experience, and the grief is real regardless of how early the loss occurs.

### How common is miscarriage after IVF?
Miscarriage rates after IVF are similar to those in natural conception and are influenced by factors such as age and embryo quality. Experiencing a miscarriage does not mean future pregnancies will also be lost.

### How can I cope after a miscarriage?
Allow yourself to grieve. There is no timeline for healing. Seek support from your partner, trusted friends, a counsellor, or a support group. Your clinic may be able to refer you to a fertility counsellor. Be gentle with yourself.

## Male Infertility

### What role does male fertility play in IVF?
Male fertility is an important part of the IVF process. Sperm quality — including count, motility, and morphology — affects fertilisation rates. A semen analysis is typically one of the first tests done when a couple begins fertility investigations.

### What if sperm quality is low?
If sperm quality is a concern, techniques such as ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection) may be used, where a single sperm is injected directly into an egg. This can significantly improve fertilisation rates in cases of male factor infertility.

### Is male infertility common?
Male factor infertility contributes to approximately half of all infertility cases. It is a medical condition, not a reflection of masculinity or worth. Many men find it helpful to speak with a counsellor or support group.

## Donor Eggs and Sperm

### What is donor egg IVF?
Donor egg IVF uses eggs from another woman (the donor) to create embryos. It is an option for people who cannot use their own eggs due to age, low ovarian reserve, genetic conditions, or other reasons. The recipient carries the pregnancy.

### What is donor sperm IVF?
Donor sperm IVF uses sperm from a donor to fertilise eggs. It is an option for single women, same-sex female couples, or couples where the male partner has no viable sperm.

### What should I know about using donor eggs or sperm?
Using donor eggs or sperm is a deeply personal decision. Many people have complex feelings about it, including grief for the genetic connection they hoped for. These feelings are completely valid. Counselling is strongly recommended before and during the process. Children born from donor conception can lead full, healthy lives.

## PCOS and IVF

### What is PCOS and how does it affect IVF?
Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a hormonal condition that affects the ovaries. People with PCOS often have many small follicles and may have irregular periods. In IVF, PCOS can mean a higher number of eggs are produced during stimulation, which also increases the risk of OHSS.

### Can people with PCOS have successful IVF?
Yes. Many people with PCOS have successful IVF outcomes. The stimulation protocol is carefully adjusted to reduce the risk of OHSS while optimising egg collection. Your doctor will tailor the approach to your specific situation.

## Endometriosis and IVF

### What is endometriosis and how does it affect fertility?
Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus. It can affect fertility by impacting egg quality, fallopian tube function, or the uterine environment. The severity varies greatly between individuals.

### Can people with endometriosis have successful IVF?
Yes. IVF can be an effective option for people with endometriosis-related infertility. The approach may be adjusted based on the extent of the condition. Your doctor will discuss the best strategy for your situation.

## Emotional Concerns

### Is it normal to feel anxious or overwhelmed during IVF?
Absolutely. IVF is physically demanding, emotionally intense, and often uncertain. Feelings of anxiety, sadness, hope, fear, and exhaustion are all completely normal. You are not alone in feeling this way.

### How can I support my mental health during IVF?
- Be honest with yourself and your partner about how you are feeling
- Consider speaking with a fertility counsellor — many clinics offer this service
- Connect with others going through IVF through support groups or online communities
- Set boundaries around how much you discuss your treatment with others
- Allow yourself rest and small pleasures
- Remember that asking for help is a sign of strength

### What if my relationship is under strain?
IVF can put significant pressure on relationships. It is common for partners to cope differently and to feel out of sync. Open, honest communication is important. Couples counselling can be very helpful during this time.

## Cost and Expectations

### How much does IVF cost?
IVF costs vary depending on the clinic, location, and the specific treatments required. A single cycle typically includes consultation fees, medication costs, laboratory fees, and the transfer procedure. Additional costs may apply for tests, frozen embryo transfers, or donor materials.

It is important to get a clear breakdown of costs from your clinic before starting treatment. Ask about what is and is not included in the quoted price.

### How many cycles of IVF might I need?
There is no fixed answer — it depends on individual factors including age, diagnosis, and response to treatment. Some people conceive on the first cycle; others require multiple attempts. Your doctor can give you a realistic picture based on your specific circumstances, but cannot guarantee outcomes.

### What are realistic expectations for IVF success?
IVF success rates vary significantly based on age, diagnosis, and clinic. Younger patients generally have higher success rates. It is important to have honest conversations with your doctor about what is realistic for your situation. Success rates are population statistics — they cannot predict what will happen for any individual person.
